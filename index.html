Endangered Marine Species

Critically Endangered

North Atlantic Right Whale

Only ~340 individuals remain. Major threats include ship strikes, fishing gear entanglement, and habitat degradation due to climate change.

Endangered

Hawksbill Sea Turtle

Population has declined by 80% over three generations. Threatened by illegal trade, coastal development, and plastic pollution.

Vulnerable

Great White Shark

Populations have declined significantly due to overfishing, bycatch, and habitat loss. Essential for marine ecosystem balance.

Vulnerable Marine Ecosystems

Coral Reefs

Often called the "rainforests of the sea," coral reefs support approximately 25% of all marine species despite covering less than 1% of the ocean floor.

Threats: Ocean warming, acidification, pollution, overfishing

Mangrove Forests

Mangroves provide crucial nursery habitats for many marine species and protect coastlines from erosion and storm surges.

Threats: Coastal development, aquaculture, climate change

Kelp Forests

These underwater forests provide food and shelter for thousands of marine species and help mitigate climate change by absorbing CO‚ÇÇ.

Threats: Warming waters, pollution, overgrazing by sea urchins

Ocean Zones & Layers

Epipelagic Zone (0-200m)

This is the sunlit top layer of the ocean where most marine life lives. Photosynthesis occurs here, making it the foundation of most ocean food webs. Temperature ranges from near freezing to about 30¬∞C (86¬∞F).

Marine Life: Phytoplankton, zooplankton, most fish, sea turtles, marine mammals

Mesopelagic Zone (200-1000m)

Also known as the twilight zone, this layer receives minimal sunlight. Many creatures here have evolved bioluminescence to communicate, find prey, and avoid predators. Pressure increases significantly with depth.

Marine Life: Lanternfish, hatchetfish, viperfish, squid, and many bioluminescent species

Bathypelagic Zone (1000-4000m)

The midnight zone is completely dark except for the light produced by organisms themselves. The pressure is crushing, and temperatures are near freezing. Food is scarce, so many animals have large mouths and sharp teeth to capture any available prey.

Marine Life: Anglerfish, gulper eels, deep-sea jellyfish, and giant squid

Abyssopelagic Zone (4000-6000m)

The abyssal zone is characterized by near-freezing temperatures, extremely high pressure, and complete darkness. Despite harsh conditions, life persists around hydrothermal vents that provide chemical energy rather than sunlight-based energy.

Marine Life: Deep-sea cucumbers, sea pigs, tripod fish, vent crabs, and tube worms

Hadalpelagic Zone (6000m+)

This zone includes the deepest ocean trenches. The pressure is over 1000 times standard atmospheric pressure at sea level. Life here is sparse but specialized, with organisms adapted to extreme conditions.

Marine Life: Snailfish, amphipods, foraminifera, and extremophile bacteria
